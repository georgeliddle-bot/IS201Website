What Is Esports?

Esports (electronic sports) refers to organized, competitive video gaming — played at an amateur or professional level, often in front of large live and online audiences. What started as small local tournaments in the 1970s has grown into a global industry worth billions of dollars, featuring full-time athletes, professional coaching staffs, dedicated training facilities, and international championships broadcast to tens of millions of viewers worldwide.

Major universities now offer esports scholarships, and games like League of Legends, Valorant, CS2, and Dota 2 boast player bases in the hundreds of millions. The 2023 League of Legends World Championship drew a peak concurrent viewership of over 6 million — rivaling traditional sporting events.

Top Esports Titles

These are the games that define modern competitive gaming, each building its own unique competitive ecosystem:

  1. League of Legends — Riot Games (2009)
    • 180+ million registered accounts worldwide
    • Annual World Championship (Worlds) draws over 70 million viewers
    • Features a franchised regional league system across NA, EU, Korea, China, and more
    • 2023 World Championship held in Seoul, South Korea
  2. Counter-Strike 2 (CS2) — Valve (2023, successor to CS:GO)
    • Oldest and most storied first-person shooter esport
    • Major tournaments carry $1 million+ prize pools
    • Hosted by PGL, ESL, and BLAST — venues fill 15,000+ seat arenas
    • Famous for its skill ceiling: professional play requires thousands of hours of practice
  3. Valorant — Riot Games (2020)
    • Grew from 0 to one of the top esports titles in under three years
    • VCT (Valorant Champions Tour) runs a global franchise circuit
    • 2023 Champions final in Los Angeles: over 1 million concurrent viewers
    • Praised for its agent ability system combined with precise gunplay
  4. Dota 2 — Valve (2013)
    • Home of The International — esports' richest annual tournament
    • 2021 prize pool exceeded $40 million, funded by the community
    • Known for extreme strategic depth and long match durations
    • Beloved in Eastern Europe, Southeast Asia, and China
  5. FIFA / EA Sports FC — EA Sports
    • Bridges traditional sports fans and gaming audiences
    • eSports World Cup and eWorld Cup attract casual and pro players alike
    • Real-life clubs like PSG, Man City, and Bayern Munich field esports rosters
    • Massive streaming presence on Twitch and YouTube

The Esports Industry by the Numbers

$1.8B Global Revenue (2023)
540M Global Viewers
$40M+ Largest Prize Pool (TI)
3.2B Gamers Worldwide

The global gaming market surpassed $200 billion in 2023, dwarfing the combined revenues of the film and music industries. Esports prize money, sponsorships, media rights, and merchandise continue to grow year over year. North America and Asia Pacific lead in both viewership and revenue, while Latin America and the Middle East represent the fastest-growing markets.
